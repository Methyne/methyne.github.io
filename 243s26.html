Structure

Accessing Materials: Grades can be viewed on Canvas, grade breakdowns for any quizzes and exams you take are available on Gradescope, homeworks are done via WebAssign, announcements are via Canvas, and all other materials are on this page. This includes discussion worksheets, solutions to (worksheets, quizzes, exams), class overview, summary of what happens each day, and extra files.

Before Discussion: Try the problems on the discussion worksheet before class. Worksheets can be downloaded here at least 2 days before the discussion.

Discussion Agenda: Once discussion starts, you can ask questions about any problems you had trouble with. You can also ask about any topics in the problems that were confusing or didn't seem to be covered by prior materials. If there are no more questions, I'll select 1-2 additional problems to cover before ending. When I go over the solution to a problem, I pause after a crucial step to give students chances to ask for further clarification before moving onto the next step.

Before Lecture: Learn any material which is prior to the material for the lecture day. Find which source(s), whether textbook, Paul's Online Math Notes, worksheets, or something else, you learn best from, and use your source(s) to catch up. Multivariable Calculus often builds on top of itself, so not knowing earlier content means you may get confused by new content.

Lecture Agenda: Instructors will cover new material along with simple example problems to illustrate it, with the problem selection and pacing up to their own styles. The topics of the day are listed in the "week of ..." schedules. What exactly will happen in detail, I am not some magic genie to be able to tell you. Contact your instructor for further info on what happens during lectures, or just come to the lectures and see for yourself.

Worksheet Info: Worksheets are posted at least 2 days in advance of discussion. Solutions are posted the end of Thursday the same week. The solution document may be dry compared to the richness of talking through every step of a solution in discussion, so email me if you missed discussion, are reading a solution in the document, and don't understand some step or the motivation to take that step.

Homework Info: Homeworks are done on WebAssign. They are automatically graded and grades appear in Canvas. If you have any trouble with the site, contact me or the instructor. Many issues with the WebAssign not accepting an answer can often be resolved by checking for syntax and type errors. You may request an automatic 24 hour deadline extension through WebAssign, but after that extensions are not guaranteed. Since homeworks are easier than quizzes and exams, note that acing the homeworks is no indicator that you'll ace the class.

Quiz Info: All sections have lecture quizzes in person during your lecture time. There are 4 lecture quizzes and they occur on 2/20, 3/13, 4/10, 5/8. The lowest lecture quiz score will be dropped. Furthermore, pop quizzes appear during lectures. All quizzes are a mix of short answer, long answer, and calculation questions. Legible scratch work can be used to award partial credit, so make your writing bright and clear.

Exam Info: All exams are in person. Midterm 1 is 3/2 from 6:30-8:30pm in Smith TBD, midterm 2 is 4/20 from 6:30-8:30pm in Smith TBD, and the final is 5/20 from 4:30-6:30pm in room TBD. Exams are no calculator, no items, final destination only. Midterm 2 covers only material between it and midterm 1, but the final is cumulative. The final will replace your lowest midterm score if your grade is higher on it.

Policies

Grade Weights:
WebAssign homework 10%, midterms 40%, lecture quizzes 15%, final exam 23%, pop quizzes 8%, course participation 4%
All midterms have equal weight, so each one is worth 40/2 = 20%. Similarly, we hold it self-evident that all lecture quizzes and pop quizzes are created equal with liberty and justice for all. On the other hand, some homeworks are more equal than others. For example, one may be worth 19 points while the next one is literally 84 points.

Cutoffs: Gaze upon each letter along with the minimum grade needed to guarantee it: A (92), A- (88), B+ (84), B (80), B- (77), C+ (74), C (71), C- (68), D+ (65), D (62), D- (59), F (-∞).

Extra Credit: If your grade is below the cutoff, then whether you can reach the grade is at the full discretion of your instructor. Lock in as early as possible before you get flummoxed. Don't expect an exception to be made or surprise extra credit to pop up. Mostly likely, there will be little to no extra credit because grades are already naturally being inflated by the weights towards freebies like participation and homework.

Assessment Taking and Grading: When you are taking a quiz or exam, the following are forbidden: calculators, note sheets, AI, help from electronic devices, phone a friend, ask the audience, 50/50. These constitute cheating and you will get a 0. Depending on the severity of your infraction, you may be knocked to the ground by an elderly man, who will banish you to the land of Yi for eternity if you do not defeat him in a game of Mahjong. Exam and quiz scores are available on Gradescope, and you have until whatever deadline your instructor announces to mention any concerns and/or request a regrade.

Makeups: For exam or lecture quiz makeups, contact the instructor as soon as you know you're going to miss the exam; the earlier, the better. You may contact either me or your instructor about WebAssign deadlines. Note that WebAssign has an automatic 24 hour extension, so do not take further extensions for granted.

Attendance: It's not clear exactly how the 4% gets divvied up. The instructors track lecture attendance. Most likely, the instructors won't calculate it in a strict way. As long as you make an effort to show up to almost everything, your attendance grade will be 100%.

Logistical remarks: The last half of Multivariable Calculus is jam packed with new concepts and tricky problems. If you fall behind in February or March, you have ample time, but if you fall behind in April or May, it will be hard to catch up. To avoid procrastinating, start the homework and worksheets early. Since attendance is a tiny portion of the total grade, you can get away with skipping things and going off to find a treasure or paint a fence instead. At the same time, this kind of Tom Sawyer toomfoolery may hurt your understanding, leading to worse exam and quiz grades. This isn't high school, you are adults, I am not yo mama, yada yada etc. In conclusion, skip classes at your own risk. Finally, if you are doing poorly in the class, then the best time to study was before you started doing poorly. The second best time to study is now. As JFK once famously never said, ask not what your instructor can do for your grade, but ask what you can do for your grade.
