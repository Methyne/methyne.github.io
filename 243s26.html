Discussion Structure

Before Discussion: Try the problems on the discussion worksheet before class. Worksheets can be downloaded here at least 3 days before the discussion.

Discussion Agenda: A 10 minute grace period is given at the start for those who haven't had a chance to look at the worksheet. Please try your best to do the worksheet before coming to discussion because you can't get much done in 10 minutes, and in some cases, not even if you had half the class. Towards the last half of the course, this courtesy may be eliminated due to the length and complexity of the problems. After review begins, you can ask questions about any problems you had trouble with. You can also ask about any topics in the problems that were confusing or didn't seem to be covered by prior materials. If there are no more questions, I'll select 1-2 additional problems to cover before ending. When I go over the solution to a problem, I pause after a crucial step to give students chances to ask for further clarification before moving onto the next step.

Policies

Discussion Attendance: Attendance will be taken every week after the first week. At the start of class, I will pass out an attendance sheet to fill. Attendance is worth 4% of the course grade.

Other Policies: Other policies may be viewed in the syllabus or on Canvas. If you have any questions about policies not pertaining to discussions, contact your instructor.

Logistical remarks: The last half of Multivariable Calculus is jam packed with new concepts and tricky problems. If you fall behind in February or March, you have ample time, but if you fall behind in April or May, it will be hard to catch up. To avoid procrastinating, start the homework and worksheets early. Since discussion attendance is a tiny portion of the total grade, you can get away with skipping things and going off to find a treasure or paint a fence instead. At the same time, this kind of Tom Sawyer toomfoolery may hurt your understanding, leading to worse exam and quiz grades. This isn't high school, you are adults, I am not yo mama, yada yada etc. In conclusion, skip classes at your own risk. Finally, if you are doing poorly in the class, then the best time to study was before you started doing poorly. The second best time to study is now. As JFK once famously never said, ask not what your instructor can do for your grade, but ask what you can do for your grade.

Other Materials

Extra Practice Material: Paul's Math Notes and the 243 Summer Site are two good sources. If you don't understand some topic or concept, Paul works from the basics and his notes may help. If you need to drill easy and medium problems to build your understanding of some area and dig yourself out of a problem solving hole, Paul has an endless supply of such problems. If you understand a tool, but need more practice applying it on harder problems, then consider the summer problems, most notably the quizzes and exams, since they are in a unique style not found elsewhere. If you want to know where a theorem or result comes from, and don't like being told something is true without knowing why it's true, then the summer site is the right place for you. Lecture notes and videos go in depth into various derivations.
